Call 999 for police, fire, or ambulance in emergencies: severe injuries, chest pain, difficulty breathing, unconsciousness, severe bleeding, or when someone's life is at risk.
üìã When to Call 999

Call 999 when:

  • Someone is seriously ill or injured
  • Someone's life is at risk
  • Severe chest pain or difficulty breathing
  • Unconsciousness or unresponsive
  • Heavy bleeding that won't stop
  • Severe burns or scalds
  • Suspected heart attack or stroke
  • Serious accidents

üå± Quick Self-Care Tips

Simple techniques to help in the moment:

ü´Å Breathing

4-7-8: Breathe in for 4, hold for 7, out for 8. Repeat 4 times to calm anxiety.

üßä Grounding

5-4-3-2-1: Name 5 things you see, 4 you touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste.

üö∂ Movement

A 5-minute walk releases endorphins and breaks negative thought cycles.

üíß Hydration

Drink water regularly. Dehydration affects mood more than you think.

üì± Digital Break

If social media makes you feel worse, take a break. Delete apps for a day.

ü§ù Talk

Share your feelings. Call someone or use the helplines above.

üîí Your Privacy Matters

When you contact support services, here's what you should know:

  • Most services are completely confidential - they won't tell anyone you called
  • Many services are anonymous - you don't have to give your name
  • University services are bound by confidentiality unless there's serious risk of harm
  • NHS records are confidential - GPs won't tell your parents if you're over 16
  • Sexual health clinics are completely confidential regardless of age
  • You can ask about confidentiality policies before sharing information
